CoFlow: Coordinated Few-Step Flow

Offline multi-agent trajectory generation in 1-3 denoising steps, without giving up coordination.

Conclusion: CoFlow moves coordination into the velocity field, so few-step inference remains coordinated.

Today I will present CoFlow, a coordinated few-step flow model for offline multi-agent decision making. The short version is this: generative models are very strong for offline multi-agent reinforcement learning, but they are usually slow because sampling takes many denoising steps. CoFlow shows that we can reduce sampling to one to three steps while preserving the cross-agent coordination that makes these models useful in the first place.

The motivation starts from a practical bottleneck. In offline MARL, the data often contains several plausible joint strategies. Generative models are attractive because they can represent these multimodal joint-action distributions. The problem is that strong diffusion and flow baselines normally generate trajectories through many iterative steps. At each step, the model has to reason across agents. So the runtime cost grows with both the number of agents and the number of sampling steps. This is a real deployment issue for cooperative control.
The trade-off

Naive few-step acceleration removes the communication path.

Naive acceleration

Distill the joint model into independent students, or run one-step generation per agent.

This reduces steps, but removes inference-time communication.

What goes wrong

Coordination cannot be recovered after the velocity field has been factorized.

Single-pass inference has no repeated attention rounds to repair missing couplings.

Conclusion: Once the velocity field is factorized by agent, one-step inference cannot recover coordination afterward.

Existing few-step routes create a coordination trade-off. One route distills a joint teacher into per-agent students. Another route applies a single-agent one-step model independently to each agent. Both are efficient, but both weaken the very mechanism that made the joint generator strong. Multi-step samplers can accumulate cooperation over repeated attention rounds. In one-step inference, that repeated communication is gone. So if the velocity field itself is not jointly coupled, coordination is already lost.

The paper contributes three main pieces. Core idea

Make the one-step velocity field natively joint-coupled.

CoFlow decomposes generation into agent-wise trajectory modeling plus a learned coordination correction.

u_theta^i = u_ind^i + u_coord^i

The coordination term is not distilled afterward. It is built directly into the averaged velocity field.

Conclusion: CoFlow treats coordination as a first-class term inside the velocity prediction.

CoFlow takes the opposite approach. Instead of first learning a joint generator and then compressing it into independent agents, we build coordination into the velocity field from the beginning. For each agent, the predicted velocity has two parts: an individual term and a coordination correction. The individual term captures that agent's own trajectory tendency. The coordination correction captures teammate information. This is the key design choice: in one forward pass, the model still has a native path for cross-agent communication.
Architecture

CVA inserts coordination at every U-Net skip connection.

Coordinated Velocity Attention

Shared query, key, and value projections exchange information across agents inside the velocity network.

Adaptive coordination gating

Each gate starts at zero, so training begins as an independent-agent baseline and turns on coupling only when useful.

Weight-shared temporal U-Net with Coordinated Velocity Attention and a shared inverse-dynamics head.

Conclusion: The model starts independent, then learns exactly where cross-agent coupling helps.

Architecturally, CoFlow uses a weight-shared temporal U-Net. The important addition is Coordinated Velocity Attention, or CVA. We insert CVA at every skip connection, so cross-agent information is available at multiple resolutions of the trajectory representation. We also add an adaptive coordination gate at each layer. The gate is initialized to zero. This matters because the model does not have to coordinate by default. It starts from a stable independent baseline and learns to activate cross-agent exchange only where the gradient signal supports it.
Training

Train the averaged velocity without second-order memory.

Why this matters

The standard consistency target uses a Jacobian-vector product, which is memory-heavy for multi-agent trajectories.

JVP target: u(z,r,t) approx v_cond - (t-r) * d u / d t

CoFlow surrogate

Two ordinary forward passes replace second-order backpropagation through the velocity field.

V_theta = u(z,0,r) + (t-r) stopgrad[u(z,0,t)-u(z,0,r)]

Conclusion: The finite-difference target keeps consistency training feasible for multi-agent scale.

The second part is training. To make one-step or few-step generation work, the averaged velocity field must be accurate. A standard consistency formulation involves a Jacobian-vector product through the velocity network, and backpropagating through that object is memory-expensive at multi-agent scale. CoFlow replaces it with a finite-difference consistency surrogate. We use two ordinary forward passes, detach the finite-difference correction, and backpropagate only through the reference-time velocity. This keeps the training objective practical on a single GPU while still nudging the model toward a consistent averaged velocity.

Conclusion: CoFlow's coordination correction is controlled by learned gates and feature diversity.

The theory formalizes this design. We show that the joint velocity can be written as per-agent velocity terms plus a coordination correction. The size of that correction is controlled by two quantities that we can inspect after training: the learned gate magnitude and the diversity of cross-agent features. This gives the method a useful interpretation. CoFlow is not simply adding capacity. It is adding a controlled coordination channel, and we can measure whether that channel is actually being used.

Conclusion: Turning the coordination channel up or down produces measurable behavioral changes.

To check whether the improvement really comes from coordination, we run three coordination probes. First, we sweep the CVA gate scale. If CVA is a meaningful coordination mechanism, changing the gate should change coordinated behavior. That is what we observe. Second, we measure landmark coverage, where agents need to spread rather than collapse onto the same target. Third, we compare CVA-on and CVA-off variants. These probes show that the performance gains are tied to inter-agent exchange, not merely to extra per-agent capacity.
Diagnostics

Learned attention and gates adapt to the task.

Learned cross-agent attention matrices show nontrivial interaction patterns.

Conclusion: CVA learns structured, task-dependent coordination rather than a uniform residual shortcut.

The learned diagnostics also support the coordination story. The attention matrices are not uniform placeholders. They show structured cross-agent interaction patterns that differ across environments. The learned gates also adapt by task. For example, coverage tasks and predator-prey pursuit tasks prefer different early-layer gate signs. This is exactly what we would expect if the gate is controlling a task-dependent coordination correction rather than acting as a generic residual scale.

Takeaway

Efficiency and coordination do not have to be opposing goals.

CoFlow makes few-step offline MARL possible by moving coordination into the velocity field itself.

1. Native joint coupling

CVA preserves cross-agent communication in one forward pass.

2. Practical training

Finite differences avoid memory-heavy second-order graphs.

3. Few-step deployment

One to three denoising steps are enough across the benchmark suite.

Final conclusion: CoFlow reaches strong offline MARL performance in 1-3 denoising steps by making coordination native to the generator.

To conclude, CoFlow shows that the apparent efficiency-coordination trade-off is not necessary. The main requirement is that coordination must live inside the one-step velocity field, rather than being accumulated only through repeated sampling or removed by per-agent factorization. With Coordinated Velocity Attention, adaptive gates, and a finite-difference consistency surrogate, CoFlow reaches strong offline MARL performance with one to three denoising steps. Thank you.
